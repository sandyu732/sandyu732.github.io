Breach case study2022

Uber MFA fatigue breach

A breach with almost no exploit code at all — just patience, persistence, and a chain of human trust decisions.

An attacker (later reported to be an 18-year-old affiliated with the Lapsus$ group) compromised an Uber external contractor's credentials, likely purchased from a dark-web marketplace after a malware infection, and used them to attempt VPN login.

MFA fatigue, precisely

The contractor had push-notification MFA enabled. The attacker triggered login attempts repeatedly, generating a flood of approval prompts on the victim's phone late at night. When simple volume didn't work, the attacker contacted the employee directly over WhatsApp, posing as Uber IT support, and told them to accept the prompt to make the notifications stop. It worked.

Why push notifications are the weak factor here
Push MFA proves the user has their phone and taps a button — it never proves the user understood what they were approving. That's a fundamentally different guarantee than a phishing-resistant factor like a FIDO2 hardware key, which cryptographically can't complete a handshake with the wrong origin no matter how many times the user is asked. See the full MFA comparison on the auth & identity page.

What turned VPN access into a full compromise

Once on the internal network, the attacker found a PowerShell script sitting on a network share with hardcoded administrator credentials for Uber's Thycotic privileged-access-management (PAM) tool — the very system meant to broker and rotate privileged credentials. That single hygiene failure escalated "one contractor's VPN session" into access across AWS, Google Workspace, and Slack, where the attacker posted screenshots publicly.

Interview framing

This breach is a good answer to "what's the weakest link in a strong-looking security posture" — Uber had MFA everywhere, and still lost, because the failure wasn't cryptographic. It was a UX decision (push notifications trust a tap, not comprehension) compounded by an operational one (secrets living in a plaintext script instead of the vault they were meant to protect). See detection & IR for how anomalous MFA-prompt volume itself is a detectable signal.
