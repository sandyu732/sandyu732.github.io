Interview technique cheat sheet

Distilled from the original interview notes this whole site is built on. This matters as much as domain knowledge.

Per-question checklist

  1. Listen fully, take notes on the question.
  2. Repeat the question back — checks understanding, buys thinking time. "Let me repeat that back to make sure I've got it…"
  3. Ask clarifying questions even if you think you know the answer — nuance often changes the right approach.
  4. State your assumptions explicitly — "I'll assume we're collecting X/Y/Z logs and retaining them 90 days…" The interviewer will correct you if it's unreasonable, and that's useful signal either way.
  5. Narrate your thinking continuously. The interviewer can only evaluate what you say, not what you silently know. If stuck: "I don't know exactly, but if I had to invent it, it would look like…" or "I know a related area well — can I talk about that instead?"
  6. For system-design/security-design prompts: draw the architecture, mark trust boundaries, apply STRIDE out loud.
  7. For coding: write pseudocode first, state complexity, write your own test cases before running them mentally against your solution.
  8. Close by asking your interviewer a prepared question — asking about "the worst parts of the job" is genuinely good; it signals confidence and gets you real signal back.

Interviewers test two different things

Depth
There will be questions about technical details to the point where it'll be hard to answer. That's okay — try your best to work through it and say what you're thinking. Interviewers often aren't looking for one specific answer; they want to see how deeply you know a topic. Approach from a low level, and check with your interviewer if you need to add more detail before moving on.
Breadth
There will be questions related to the role, and some that aren't — this explores range. Explore the scenario, ask questions, and say your thinking aloud; you might be on the right track even when it doesn't feel like it.

The current Google loop (2026)

Google's interview process has changed since the underlying study notes were written. As of 2026: a return to in-person interviews to counter AI-assisted cheating, a pre-loop "Google Hiring Assessment" screen, and more AI/ML system design questions woven into onsite loops. A Security Engineering onsite typically runs up to ~6 rounds of 45–60 minutes: coding/data structures, one or two system-design-style rounds (often with a security angle — "design a secure X"), and a Googleyness/Leadership behavioral round. Confirm your specific loop structure with your recruiter.

The core principle
Interviewers are potential friends and they want to help you get the job — they are on your side. Let them help you, ask them questions, recite everything you know on a topic, and say your thought process out loud.
