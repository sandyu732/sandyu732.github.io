Networking & TLS

Table stakes on the core stack, real depth on the TLS 1.3 handshake — this is one of the highest-yield topics in the whole loop, because it touches crypto, networking, and PKI at once.

Core stack — know cold, don't over-invest

This is table stakes, not where depth is scored. Be fast and precise, then move on.

Where to go deep: the TLS 1.3 handshake

Narrate this as a story, not a checklist: the client says what it supports, the server picks and proves its identity, and both sides derive the same symmetric key without ever transmitting it.

TLS 1.2 vs 1.3

1.3 cut the handshake from 2-RTT to 1-RTT by having the client guess the server's preferred key-exchange group and send its key share immediately, instead of negotiating the cipher suite first. 1.3 also removed static RSA key exchange, CBC ciphers, and renegotiation entirely — every 1.3 handshake now provides forward secrecy by mandate, not by configuration choice.

Key exchange: ECDHE, and why ephemeral is the load-bearing word

ECDHE (elliptic-curve Diffie-Hellman, ephemeral) is standard. A new keypair per session means a compromised long-term private key still can't decrypt past traffic — that's forward secrecy. Contrast with static RSA key exchange (pre-TLS-1.3), where a stolen private key retroactively decrypts every recorded session. See the Heartbleed case study for exactly how that distinction played out in practice.

0-RTT resumption risk
TLS 1.3 supports 0-RTT via pre-shared keys from a prior session, but 0-RTT data has no replay protection — a captured 0-RTT ClientHello can be replayed. Only wrap idempotent requests in 0-RTT.

Certificate verification chain

Leaf certificate → intermediate CA(s) → trusted root, shipped in the OS/browser trust store. Verification checks: signature validity up the chain, expiry, revocation (OCSP/CRL, and OCSP stapling to avoid a separate round trip), hostname match against the SAN field (not just CN — CN-only matching was deprecated), and certificate transparency log inclusion, which Chrome has required since 2018 to catch mis-issued certs. See the DigiNotar case study for what happens when this chain is trusted a little too much.

Downgrade and implementation-flaw attacks

POODLE
Exploited SSL 3.0's padding-oracle weakness in CBC mode, forced via protocol downgrade.
BEAST
Chosen-plaintext attack on CBC IV predictability in TLS 1.0.
CRIME / BREACH
Compression-ratio side channel leaks secrets embedded in compressed responses.
Heartbleed
OpenSSL buffer over-read via a malformed heartbeat length field — a memory-safety bug, not a protocol flaw. Full deep dive on the breach page.

2025–2026 update: post-quantum key exchange is live, not theoretical

NIST finalized ML-KEM (FIPS 203, from CRYSTALS-Kyber) in August 2024 as the standard post-quantum key-encapsulation mechanism. Chrome and Google's infrastructure have already deployed hybrid key exchange (X25519 + ML-KEM) in TLS 1.3 — hybrid because a classical break of ML-KEM alone shouldn't compromise security, and a future quantum break of X25519 alone shouldn't either.

Why now, if quantum computers don't exist yet
"Harvest now, decrypt later": an adversary recording encrypted traffic today could decrypt it once a cryptographically relevant quantum computer exists. Long-lived-secrecy traffic needs PQC now, not after that computer arrives.

Practice questions

  • Walk through what happens, conceptually byte-by-byte, when you connect to https://google.com.
  • Why does forward secrecy matter if TLS is already encrypted?
  • Why would Google deploy hybrid PQC key exchange instead of waiting for a pure post-quantum standard to mature?
  • What's the difference between an out-of-bounds read and an out-of-bounds write, and which one was Heartbleed?
