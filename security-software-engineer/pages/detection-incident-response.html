Tier 2 — working knowledge

Detection & incident response

The detection pipeline, IOCs vs behavioral detection, response models, and a scenario to practice out loud.

Signal generation → triage → alerting pipeline

IOCs vs behavioral detection

IOCs (known-bad IPs, hashes, domains) are fast but brittle — trivially evaded by changing infrastructure. Behavioral/anomaly detection (unusual login times, unusual data volume, unusual process ancestry) is more resilient but noisier and needs a good baseline. A role suddenly issuing high-volume calls across resources it doesn't normally touch — exactly what happened undetected for months in the Capital One breach — is a strong anomaly signal that static policy review alone would never catch.

Incident response models

SANS PICERL
Preparation, Identification, Containment, Eradication, Recovery, Lessons learned — the industry-standard mental model.
Google's IMAG
Incident Management At Google, modeled on FEMA's incident command system. Google runs IR with a formal incident-commander structure — worth mentioning to show you've looked past generic frameworks toward how Google specifically operates.

Practice scenario, out loud

Prompt
"You get an alert that a service account exfiltrated 2GB to an external IP at 3am. Walk me through what you do."

Structure your answer: clarify scope → contain (revoke/rotate credentials, isolate host) → identify root cause and blast radius → eradicate → recover → postmortem/lessons-learned. Explicitly mention why you wouldn't tip off the attacker prematurely, and when you'd loop in legal/comms.
