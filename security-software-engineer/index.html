Reality check — read this first
This covers roughly 16 domains — enough for a multi-month plan. Tier 1 (5 pages) is where real depth is scored in a Senior SWE Security loop: go deep, derive from first principles, don't just recite definitions. Tier 2 (3 pages) needs solid working knowledge — hold a 15-minute conversation, reason through a scenario. Tier 3 (3 pages) needs conversational fluency only — know the shape well enough to not go blank and to ask smart clarifying questions. Depth on five topics beats shallow coverage of sixteen.
The 2026 Google loop
Google is shifting back toward in-person interviews, added a pre-loop "Google Hiring Assessment" screen, and is weaving more AI/ML system design questions into onsite loops. A Security Engineering onsite typically runs up to ~6 rounds: coding/data structures, one or two security-design rounds, and a Googleyness/Leadership round. Confirm your specific loop with your recruiter. Full checklist on the interview technique page.

Suggested schedule

WhenFocus
Day 1 (weekend)Networking & TLS + Cryptography, deep. 2 coding problems in the evening.
Day 2 (weekend)Web app security + Auth & identity, deep. 2 more coding problems, then explain 3 Tier 1 topics out loud, timed.
Day 3 (evening)Threat modeling + Cloud & infra security.
Day 4 (evening)Detection & incident response + 1–2 coding problems.
Day 5 (evening)Tier 3 breadth pass: malware, exploits, attack structure. Revisit anything still shaky.
Day 6 (evening)Digital forensics breadth pass, then one full mock interview: a coding problem plus a security-design prompt, talked through start to finish.
Day 7 / day beforeLight review only. Walk the flashcard deck once more. Re-read the interview technique page. Sleep beats one more hour of cramming.
How to study each page
Cover the term, guess the answer first, then check yourself. Say it out loud as if explaining to an interviewer. Push one layer deeper than the page gives you. Connect every mechanism to an attack — the breach pages exist for exactly that.
